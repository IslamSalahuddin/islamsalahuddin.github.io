Research Methods - Unit 1: Research Methods and Professional Practice

Overview

As per the course website, "Welcome to Week 1, in which you will be introduced to Research Methods as a subject. We will start with the scientific method and ethics considerations in research. The first question to answer in this module would be “What is the Purpose of Research?”. Whether you continue in Higher Education or move into industry, it is essential to understand why you carry out research. There are 3 main reasons/purposes of research: to explore (familiarise yourself with a topic/situation), to describe (describe your findings), and to explain (answer the question “why?”)."

My Reflection

Overall Reflection

In the first unit of the module, I went through a long reading list that aims to lay out the basis of research methods and ethical principles, espcially in the AI domain context. I was glad that the listed papers are generally recent and related to the domain of the program.

There were a couple of readings covering the differences and uses of deductive and inductive research, capped with a quick quiz using examples for both. Regarding AI research ethics and principles,one of the papers introduced me to Menlo Report and how it built on Belmont Report, and how they aimed to establish an ethiacl framework for research invlolving Information and Communication Technologies. Another article was a good one, giving a literature review for 200 publications tackling AI ethics, summarising the findings in a Power BI Dashboard. It was also nice to know what it takes to be an AI ethicist.

I was also introduced to the Code of Conduct published by the BCS, The Chartered Institute for IT, which I used as a reference to evaluate on of the case studies published by the Association for Computing Machinery (ACM) write a post evaluating the ethical basis for such a case, initiating the first collaborative discussion that I have in this module.

Artefacts

Collaborative Learning Discussion 1

Initiating the first collaborative discussion for this module, we were asked to pick a case study from the examples provided by the ACM, and review the application of the ethics code to the situation described and highlight the impact on any relevant legal (jurisdictional or non-jurisdictional) and social issues, as well as on the professionalism of the computing professionals involved.

My initial post:

Using the case study of Automated Active Response Weaponry (ACM, 2024), I shall argue that the presented defence technologies business, Q Industries, began violating the British Computer Society (BCS) Code of Conduct (BCS, 2022) as soon as it allowed its vehicles to be used as defence mechanisms against human beings.

Q Industries vehicles started as “passive systems”, performing tasks like bomb diffusion (ACM, 2024). Such a task has a controlled environment, meaning that the criterion for the task’s success or failure is crystal clear and non-debatable: whether to diffuse the bomb or not. Furthermore, success in this task is clearly in the public interest, as it eliminates harm for those who would have been hit by the bomb explosion, as well as the human personnel that would have had to deal with the bomb in the absence of this technology.

However, at the moment of putting such technology in confrontation with humans, as early indicated by putting the technology to use for crowd monitoring (ACM, 2024), Q Industries stepped into muddy waters. The very act of putting human beings under surveillance without their knowledge contradicts the principle of respecting legitimate rights of relevant parties (BCS Codes 1.b. and 2.f.). Using machine learning algorithms for face detection to take decisions on whether the respective human being should be acted upon is known to have deep ethical bias considerations that can hardly be fully resolved, but are rather usually compromised (Wehrli et al., 2021).

While this may be appropriate in many application fields, it is unarguably unacceptable when it comes to harming human beings, according to basic human ethics and BCS Code 1.a (BCS, 2022), regarding regard for the wellbeing of others, and Code 1.c (BCS, 2022), regarding likely discrimination based on facial traits. On top of the previous, Q Industries agreed to expand its vehicles’ response to suspects to include lethal actions, which crucially amplifies the previously mentioned issues and concerns.

Building on the previous reasoning, the act of the resigned engineers (ACM, 2024) can be justified as a grounded act of protest and upholding of the profession’s basic ethical guidelines and principles, which is itself stated by articles a, b and c of BCS Code 4. Therefore, Q Industries’ lawsuit against its protesting engineers should be refuted as a continued disrespect from the business towards the profession’s standards and fundamental ethical principles.
