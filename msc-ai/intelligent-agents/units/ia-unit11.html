Intelligent Agents - Unit 11: Intelligent Agents in Action

Overview

As per the course website, "This unit will bring together several of the core subjects that have been covered to date, such as agent-based computing, adaptive algorithms and deep learning. This week focuses on how these technologies are currently being used in practice in manufacturing and Industry 4.0, specifically manufacturing and FinTech, to great effect."

My Reflection

Overall Reflection

The unit included a lecturecast and a set of readings that covered some of intelligent agent applications in various domains, mainly in industrial domains, tackled through the role of AI in Industry 4.0, and AI applications in specific sectors like FinTech.

The lecturecast and the readings also briefly touched on the use of deep learning in creative industries, through a paper introducing a deep learning model that predicts the next line rap lyrics line. This was the most interesting thing in the unit for me, given its relation to creative practices. I also had to notice that the paper is from 2016, which is pre-Transformers era. That is why the paper introduced a ranking deep learning model RankSVM, treating rap lyrics lines as an information retrieval problem. On the other hand, Transformers-based models generate new content based on large language models (LLMs) that are fine-tuned to rap lyrics. It was nice for me to see relevant efforts before the invention and use of Transformers, and what Transformers have enabled regarding creative practices.

Artefacts

Collaborative Discussion 3 | Cont'd

Resuming the discussion with the prompt, "The advent of new technologies supported by Deep Learning models mean that it is now possible to generate ‘new’ content, for example, Dall-E AI to generate images or ChatGPT to create prose. Do you think that these new technologies offer any ethical issues that should be considered, and if not, why not?" Below I provide a summary post for my initial post and the one response that I received.

My Summary Post

In my initial post, I posed the question of what creativity means in the age of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI). I framed my approach by saying that the advancing models can generate endless new content - whether prose, literature, paintings, music, etc. - easily in all sorts of styles, artistic schools, ideas, and tones (Sternberg, 2024), posing the question of how we can understand human creativity.

I also pointed out creativity has been long considered a capability very specific to human beings. Now, this belief is threatened with founded suspicions. Some researchers argue that ‘artificial creativity’ has its limitations - or rather, in better terms, it is inherently different than humans’. For example, GenAI has been found to be able to come up with incremental discoveries but not groundbreaking new ideas like humans (Ding and Li, 2025). Other researchers do not see the topic from the perspective of a zero-sum game and focus more on human-machine collaboration frameworks and examples (Cai and Gao, 2025).

In her peer response, Ioanna kindly extended the question I posed, pointing out that GenAI consequentially challenges the notions of originality and authorship, especially that GenAI outputs may often include recombination of existing human-created material rather than entirely novel ideas.

In response to these questions, Ioanna – like myself – chooses the side of exploring possible human-machine collaboration frameworks. Ioanna said that this is the trend in emerging studies that show the potential of GenAI in enhancing creative processes while still requiring human direction, interpretation and critical judgment (Gurel, 2025). Ioanna suggests implementing ‘transparent attribution and disclosure frameworks’ that declare the role of both human and machine contributions.

Overall, I found Ioanna’s response insightful and constructive. The attribution and disclosure frameworks that she mentioned would be worth further research.
