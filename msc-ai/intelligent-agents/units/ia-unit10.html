Collaborative Discussion 3 | Cont'd

Resuming the discussion with the prompt, "The advent of new technologies supported by Deep Learning models mean that it is now possible to generate ‘new’ content, for example, Dall-E AI to generate images or ChatGPT to create prose. Do you think that these new technologies offer any ethical issues that should be considered, and if not, why not?" Below I respond to two peer posts.

Post #1

The first post gave a summary for the ethical concerns of generative artificial intelligence (AI), counting them misinformation, amplifying societal biases, copyrights infringement and inappropriate use. I aimed to connect between his post and my initial post. Below is my response.

My Response

Your post effectively summarises the top ethical challenges that have risen with the advancement of generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI). Misinformation, amplifying societal biases, copyrights infringement and inappropriate use are all areas to question how we can reap the benefits of GenAI while navigating the risks of it (Kumar and Poonam, 2025; Sivagnanam et al, 2025).

The risk that is most relevant to the one that I chose is the one related to copyrights, or rather the risks related to GenAI's ability to 'create'. Since GenAI can create new content in all forms (Sternberg, 2024), a question rises regarding what human creativity means in this new era. After computers took over the advantage on the front of computational capabilities since the 1970s, creativity has long been considered a capability very specific to human beings. Now, this belief is threatened with founded suspicions.

However, adding your concern to mine, the issue becomes double folded. GenAI is shaking the claim of uniqueness of human creativity, while it is also generating content that is based on humans' content in the first place, of which copyrights might be violated GenAI content.

I think the answer to your concern and mine - and to them both combined - can be mainly either of two approaches. One is to disregard AI-generated content and continue to wage wars to prohibit the training of generative deep learning models on human artefacts, with all the doubts of what that will lead to. The second is to shift our focus to build more robust and sustainable human-machine collaboration frameworks, which would require a revisit to all of our standing concepts, including copyrights, AI companies' legal guardrails and what creativity means (Cai and Gao, 2025).

Post #2

In a second post, a colleague chose a very specific approach, mainly about the ethical challenges of using generative deep learning models, specifically GANs, to produce synthetic data in the domain of official statistics. His central argument is that synthetic data is often presented as a privacy-preserving solution, but this framing is overly optimistic. He raises three key concerns: that synthetic data may still approximate real individuals closely enough to compromise anonymisation, that it can replicate or amplify biases from the original data, and that it can create a false sense of data quality that misleads policy decisions. His conclusion is that synthetic data does not resolve ethical issues but shifts ethical responsibility towards model design and governance.

My Response

Your post offers a very insightful perspective by grounding the ethical discussion in the specific domain of official statistics and synthetic data. It is a valuable addition to the discussion because it moves beyond the more commonly discussed ethical issues of generative AI, such as misinformation and copyright, and into a domain where the stakes directly affect public policy and institutional trust.

Your point about synthetic data not resolving ethical issues but rather reconfiguring them resonates strongly. It echoes a pattern that seems to apply across many applications of generative deep learning: the technology does not eliminate existing challenges but reshapes them, often in ways that are harder to detect. The concern you raise about bias replication is particularly important. Since generative models, including GANs, learn from historical data distributions, they risk encoding and perpetuating the very inequalities that official statistics should aim to expose and address (Kimberley, 2025). This aligns with the broader literature on how biases embedded in training data can propagate through AI systems in subtle but consequential ways (Kumar and Poonam, 2025).

What I find most compelling in your argument is the notion of false confidence. The idea that synthetic datasets can appear statistically sound while lacking causal fidelity is a nuanced risk that is easy to overlook when the outputs look convincing on the surface. This connects to a wider theme in the ethics of generative AI: that the ability to produce realistic content, whether data, text or images, does not inherently guarantee that the content is meaningful, accurate or fair (Figueira and Vaz, 2022).

Your conclusion that synthetic data shifts ethical responsibility towards model design and governance rather than away from it is a point that deserves emphasis across all domains of generative AI, not only official statistics.

This was a research formal activity to add to the portfolio, requiring to choose an application of Deep Learning, and research how it works and its impacts on society. Since I'm focused on the intersection between AI and creative industries, and as an extension to the collaborative discussion, I chose the application of Deep Learning in literature text generation. Below is my research article.

Deep Learning in Creative Writing: When the Agent Becomes the Author

Overview of the Technology

Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT-4, Claude, and open-source alternatives like Llama and Qwen have become increasingly capable of generating literary text, from short fiction and poetry to full-length novel drafts. These systems are now used by authors as co-writing tools, by publishers for content generation, and by individuals pursuing personal creative projects. Recent estimates suggest that 10 to 24 percent of content in areas such as business communications and consumer-facing writing now involves some degree of LLM assistance (Liang et al., 2025). The technology has moved well beyond simple autocomplete. Modern systems can maintain narrative coherence across thousands of words, adopt specific stylistic registers, and engage in iterative creative collaboration with human writers.

How It Works

At their foundation, these systems are built on the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017), which uses self-attention mechanisms to model long-range dependencies in sequential data. During training, the model learns statistical patterns across vast corpora of text, including published literature, using objectives like next-token prediction. The result is a system that generates fluent, contextually appropriate prose by sampling from learned probability distributions over language.

What makes this especially relevant to our module is the emerging paradigm of LLM-based autonomous agents. When an LLM is embedded in an agentic loop, perceiving a prompt (the creative brief), reasoning about narrative structure and style, and acting by generating text, it begins to resemble a BDI architecture (Rao and Georgeff, 1995). The model's internal representations function as beliefs about language; the user's prompt encodes desires; and the generation process can be understood as the execution of intentions. This is not a loose metaphor. It is a genuine structural parallel with the agent models we have studied (Wooldridge, 2009), and it raises the question of whether deep learning systems, when placed inside agentic loops, cross the threshold from tools into agents in any meaningful sense.

Socio-Technical Impacts

The ethical and social dimensions of this technology are layered and far-reaching. The most immediate tension concerns authorship and intellectual property. The U.S. Copyright Office maintains that copyright protection requires human authorship, which means purely AI-generated works currently cannot be copyrighted (U.S. Copyright Office, 2025). But the boundary between "AI-assisted" and "AI-generated" is not sharp. Where AI merely assists an author, its use does not change the copyrightability of the output; however, if content is entirely generated by AI, it cannot be protected (U.S. Copyright Office, 2025). For writers who co-create with these systems, this ambiguity is a real and unresolved problem.

There is also the question of training data and consent. LLMs are trained on enormous datasets that often include copyrighted literary works scraped from the internet without explicit permission. Existing legal frameworks appear ill-equipped to handle semantic and stylistic reproduction, with no clear licensing model for training data and inadequate protection for authors' creative identities (Sheridan, 2026). The ongoing litigation (for instance, Authors Guild v. OpenAI) is testing these boundaries in court.

A subtler but perhaps more profound concern is the homogenisation of creative voice. O'Sullivan (2025) found through stylometric analysis that while AI produces polished prose, its output follows narrow and uniform patterns, whereas human authors display far greater stylistic diversity shaped by personal experience and creative intent. If AI-generated text proliferates in publishing, we risk a flattening of the very diversity that makes literature valuable. The irony is worth noting: a technology trained on the full breadth of human literary expression tends to collapse that breadth into something narrower.

The labour implications are equally significant. The 2023 Hollywood screenwriters' strike prominently featured demands to restrict unregulated use of generative AI in scriptwriting, reflecting deep tensions between creators and platform providers over authorship and compensation (European Parliament, 2025). Similar pressures are building in literary ghostwriting, journalism, and content writing more broadly.

On the other hand, these tools can also democratise creative expression by lowering barriers for people who have ideas but lack the technical writing skills to realise them, supporting writers with disabilities, and enabling multilingual creative work. The technology is not uniformly harmful; the question is one of governance and intent.

Conclusion

Deep learning applied to creative writing sits at an intersection of technology, law, ethics, and art that is difficult to untangle. From an agent-based computing perspective, it raises a question that I think is genuinely provocative: when an autonomous system can produce literary output that is, in many contexts, indistinguishable from human work, what does "authorship" really mean? And by extension, what does "agency" mean? These are not only legal or technical questions. They are epistemic ones, and they connect directly to the foundational concerns of this module.
